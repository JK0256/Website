Mechanical Engineering - Rube Goldburg Machine (9/8/23-9/15/23)

We used Tinkercad.com to create an initial prototype of a Rube Goldburg machine we'll be making. We need to start the machine with a baseball and end it with a balloon popping. We plan to roll a ball tied to a string to lift a block in front of a hot wheels, then allowing the hot wheels to trigger a pulley that will drop a tungstun cube (courtesy of Duncan) to smash the balloon.


We found some issues with our original design. For example, the original trigger of the final pulley was a ring hooked to a rotating shaft (that would be pushed by the hot wheel), but we found that it would be too difficult to have the shaft rotate through the car hitting it.

We started to put together our physical product, and we decided to have a pulley hanging off of the table, rather than creating two supports for the pulley to stand up on the table. Also, we turned the ramp sideways rather than pointing it away from the pulley.

Our tungstun cube is tied using a string with a ring on the end, and the ring has a rod which is held in place by two supports so that the rod doesn't go toward the cube, but rather so that the rod is yanked sideways as the ball tied to the rod falls to the floor off of the ramp. This was made possible in part because we turned the ramp sideways.

We added another ramp for a hot wheels leading up to the ramp that the ball rolls down so that the hot wheels can hit the ball, and trigger the rest of the Rube Goldburg machine.


So now the process is: hot wheels rolls down the taller ramp, the hot wheels hits the baseball on lower ramp, baseball falls to floor and pulls the rod that its connected to by string, the rod gets pulled out of the ring that is connected to the string that is connected to the tungstun cube, and the tungstun cube falls and pops the balloon.


Thankfully, it worked on our first try!

Aerodynamics - Bottle Rockets

For our aerodynamics unit, we did another classic science project: bottle rockets! This turned out to be quite a bit more intensive than I thought it would be, and there were definitely more details than I there would be. For example: how to attach the parachute, or how to attach the cap, or how to attach the fins. There were many different ways we could do each thing, but through brainstorming and a bit of trial and error, Duncan and I created a pretty neat rocket with a plastic bag parachute inside a bottle nose, diagonally-placed fins, and quite a bit of super glue.


Unfortunately, our rocket didn't work too well. It flew relatively straight and in an arc. The diagonally-placed fins really helped with making the rocket rotate in the air while it flew. However, the nose was way too heavy because of our parachute inside it, and our rocket barely flew through the air for less than a second.

Business Engineering

The Business Engineering unit was very unlike our other subjects. Rather than hands-on engineering, we learned the basic theory behind how business and engineering mesh together. We learned about the current business engineering landscape, namely things such as the top engineering companies and the industries that required business engineers. It was quite a shock seeing how wide the range of business engineering is in today's world. We also learned about many skills, concepts, and methods needed in business engineering, such as design thinking, customer obsession, and project management.

Having learned the theory behind the engineering process, we moved to put these theories into practive. Our project for the Business Engineering unit was creating a pitch for a product (tech, home appliances, school tools, apps, etc), and presenting it to the class.

For my pitch with my partner, we created a pitch for an app that we would call PenShift. PenShift, as we presented it, was an app that would scan a handwritten piece of text and convert it into digital text. We pitched it with the educational setting in mind, thinking of its possible benefits for students and teachers. Using PenShift would allow students to avoid grueling work copying handwritten text into a digital form, and teacher would benefit by being able to convert messy student handwriting into digital text, a boon for any teacher needing to grade student assignments.

Civil Engineering

Spaghetti Bridge

  1. Our bridge weighed 46.8 grams, the heaviest in the class.
  2. Despite our high hopes, the bridge only held 1009 grams, barely breaking a kilogram.
  3. The ratio, or score, was 21.5 grams of water held per gram of bridge.
  4. The heaviest part of our bridge was the middle, because we had designed our bridge to be stronger in the center than in the sides.
  5. Our design came entirely from inspiration and speculation. Based on a project from another class, we had initially planned to make a ladder-like bridge with criss-crossing supports between two long bands, becoming a 2D plane. However, we decided to made a triangular prism by creating two of these criss-cross ladder structures and gluing them together.
  6. We had hoped that reinforcing the center would make the bridge less likely to break down the middle. For that reason, we put the carabiner in the middle when we executed our project. Unfortunately, is seemed that the bridge was too rigid and still too weak in the middle, as the bridge broke in the middle upon the test.
  7. As was aforementioned, the break point was the center of the bridge. Admittedly, we had not antipated it as we had attempted to reinforce the middle the most of all of the bridge.
  8. I believe that a structure of bridge that could accommodate several points of pressure would be much more capable of holding more weight. With the weight spread out between multiple points on the bridge, we could've more evenly distributed the downward force of the water's weight.

Chemical Engineering - Making Oobleck

As with all of our units, we first learned a bit about the subject itself: jobs in the subject, specific fields within the subject, and the various skills needed in these fields.

The project was rather simple this time, and quite fun as well! We made oobleck, a mixture of water and corn starch. Oobleck is a unique material that is hard when hit by a large force, However, it becomes like a liquid when a gentle force is exerted upon it.

We didn't know the exact water-corn starch ratio for making the ideal oobleck (we later learned it was 50-50), so we pretty much just eyeballed it. We started by putting some corn starch first, then gradually putting in water and mixing it, and just kept doing that until the oobleck was perfect. Well, except that out oobleck still ended up a bit too watery anyways. So our plan didn't really go as planned, but it was all still very interesting.
